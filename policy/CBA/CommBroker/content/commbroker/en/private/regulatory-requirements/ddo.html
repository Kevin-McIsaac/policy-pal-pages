Overview:

Where did the Design and Distribution Obligations (DDO) come from?

In November 2014, the Financial System Inquiry (FSI) found the current regulatory framework for financial products to be insufficient for consumer protection because it relies too heavily on disclosure, general advice and financial literacy of consumers.

Following the FSI’s recommendations, the Government introduced Part 7.8A and 7.9A to the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) (Corporations Act):

Product Design and Distribution Obligations which necessitate issuers and distributors of financial products (captured under the regime) to be targeted at the right people.

Product Intervention Powers (PIP) which provide ASIC the power to modify products, or if necessary ban the sale of financial products, when there is a risk of significant consumer detriment. ASIC will also have the power to impose civil and criminal penalties for any breach of DDO or PIP.

 

 

Embedding conduct into product governance

At its core, the regime is about embedding conduct throughout the entire product lifecycle. This is by:

  • shifting away from heavy reliance on disclosure, which requires customers to determine if the financial products are suitable for them, to ensuring financial products are targeted at the right people;
  • ensuring financial products offer value to the customer; and
  • strengthening product issuer and distributor accountability by introducing obligation targeted to each.

Additionally, regulatory powers are enhanced as previously ASIC had limited ability to intervene - unless the disclosure was defective.

 

DDO PRINCIPLES:

  • Fairness in designing, targeting and distributing products that meet consumer needs
  • A consumer-centric approach to the entire product lifecycle
  • DDO requirements be implemented in a flexible and scalable manner
  • Measures should not unnecessarily inhibit product innovation and prevent customers to access a diversity of products
  • Implementation of requirements in a way unnecessary regulatory costs are avoided

 

What does it mean for issuers and distributors?

DDO require issuers and distributors to develop and maintain effective product governance arrangements across the life cycle of financial products.

Brokers are considered distributors of products. More information can be found on the following pages to help meet your obligations under DDO.

 

What does it mean for the customer?

DDO is intended to help customers obtain products that are likely to be consistent with their needs, objectives and financial situation.

 

What are the key aspects of DDO for Brokers?

  1. Distribute in line with the Target Market Determination (TMD)
  • The TMD document that is publicly available on the CommBank website.
  • The Target Market and eligibility criteria.
  • Any distribution conditions noted on the TMD.
  • Review trigger information reporting requirements

Under DDO there is a prohibition not to engage in retail distribution conduct without a valid TMD. This does not apply where the distributor is engaging in ‘excluded conduct’ for the purposes of DDO. Personal Advice is classified as ‘excluded conduct’ under DDO.

  1. Record keeping and Reporting
    You must collect and keep records of distribution information, and provide this information to the issuer. The TMD sets out what should be reported and when.
    For example, complaints data and other data required as set out in the issuer’s TMD.
  2. Capture and report significant dealings
    You must notify the issuer of any significant dealings in the product that are not consistent with the TMD as soon as practicable, and within 10 business days at the latest

 

Overview of a TMD:

What’s a Target Market Determination?

A Target Market Determination is a document which describes who a product is appropriate for (target market), and any conditions around how the product can be distributed to customers. It also describes the events or circumstances where we may need to review the Target Market Determination for a financial product.

 

Why does CommBank need to have Target Market Determinations?

We’re required to have Target Market Determinations under the Treasury Laws Amendment (Design and Distribution Obligations and Product Intervention Powers) Act 2019.

This is to make sure we’re keeping customers at the centre of our approach to the design and distribution of our financial products.

There are 4 key sections within a TMD that are applicable to you when making recommendations about products to your customers- please note the full TMD will be available Target Market Determinations - CommBank

 

  1. Dates are to be updated every time a review is undertaken, with the Next Review Date to align to the Review period (relative to the start date) The Review period is designated based on the Product Risk Rating
     

  1. Outline the product’s Target Market & Key Features, including:
  • Likely objectives, financial situation and needs
  • Eligibility Criteria
  • Key Features
  • Why Product suits Target Market's needs
  • Capture negative target market (if relevant)
  • Distribution Conditions
  • Review trigger information reporting requirements

  1. Outline any conditions / restrictions when distributing the product. Distribution conditions should be considered in the Reasonable Steps Assessments.

  1. Capture minimum review triggers and identify any additional triggers that may suggest the TMD is no longer appropriate. Review triggers feed into the Reporting Plan, and form the basis for ongoing assessments of the effectiveness of the TMD
