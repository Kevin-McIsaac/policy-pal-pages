1. About this policy

 

1.1. Why this policy

As a responsible lender, we need to consider how our applicants’ request for a new residential mortgage will impact their financial position.
The Bank’s debt servicing criteria looks at the applicant(s) ability to meet all existing and proposed regular commitments (debts) and living expenses without enduring financial hardship.
This policy will help you to understand the assessment of the applicant(s) capacity to service loan in a residential mortgage application.  The following topics are covered in this section:
  • Serviceability assessment
  • Joint borrower applications
  • Common Debt Reducer (CDR)
  • Commitment Cover Ratio (CCR)
  • Benchmark, Buffer and Floor rates
  • Product Benchmark rates
  • Loan instalment calculation
  • Total commitments
  • Notional Rent
  • Household Expenditure Measure (HEM)
  • Customer declared living expenses vs HEM
  • Parental Leave Home Lending
  • Rental income tax deductions for Australian investment properties
  • Changes to financial circumstance
 
Serviceability assessment (or capacity to service loan) forms one part of the unsuitability assessment as required under our Responsible Lending obligations.
If the loan application requires mortgage insurance, you may also need to refer to Lenders mortgage insurance (LMI) for further information.
 

2. Rules you must apply


2.1 Serviceability assessment


Income


Applicant(s) income only is to be used.
 
For additional information also refer to Co- borrower applications , Parental Leave Home Lending and Verifying Income .
 
Where the applicant / borrower / CDR use income from a self-employed entity, wholly or partially owned by them towards serviceability assessment, or where the borrower / applicant / CDR’s self-employed entity made a loss, refer to Self-employed applicants policy  for further assessment requirements.
 
Where customers own a controlling interest in a company (i.e. more than 50%), and there is a shortfall in serviceability from the applicants' individual incomes, company earnings may be added to applicants’ incomes, proportionate to the level of ownership. To use income from this source you must adhere to the requirements as outlined in Self Employed Applicants policy > Applications by income source > use of undistributed company profits towards individual serviceability calculations


Living expenses


As part of serviceability assessment (including loan variations), brokers are required to capture all borrowers’ ongoing living expenses and existing debt commitments in ApplyOnline.
For the purposes of assessing an applicant’s capacity to repay the Bank, the higher of Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) and Customer Declared Expenses compared to HEM, plus customer declared living expenses not compared to HEM, will be used in serviceability assessment.

Refer to the following Rules sections:

  • Customer declared living expenses not compared to HEM

For further information refer to the following policies:
  • Living expenses

General information


The applicants’ ability to service all debts is to be assessed on a Principal and Interest (P&I) basis, taking account of the requirements for applying a benchmark rate.
This requirement is irrespective of loan purpose, interest only repayment period, LVR or security to be provided.
Serviceability assessment is to be completed on a P&I basis over the loan term requested by the applicants, for new loans up to a maximum of 30 years, as follows:
  • Residential housing or residential investment loans, including multi-sequence loans;
  • Relocation loans with end debt
Serviceability assessment is not required for Relocation loans with no end debt. 

Where a liability is being repaid in full by this loan application, the loan repayment (monthly / fortnightly etc.) is not included in serviceability calculations.



Interest only / combination loans


For Interest Only / Combination loans (i.e. interest only repayment period followed by P&I); serviceability assessment will be calculated over the residual loan amortising term (i.e. the P&I term), and not the total loan term (for both Owner Occupied and Investment loans) - refer to Assessing Maximum Loan Term .

Example:

Overall loan term of 30 years, I/O repayments for 10 years and the residual loan term of 20 years on P&I.  At loan origination, the loan commitment, serviceability assessment is calculated on a P&I basis over the amortising period i.e. 20 years in this case.
Whilst the minimum term for the interest only/combination loan is 1 year interest only converting to 1 year principal and interest, it is suggested that the P&I term should be set-up for the longest term possible.

Example:

On a $200,000 loan the P&I repayments for 1 year @ 6.57% are $17,271 per month and for 25 years $1,365 per month.


Linked loan applications

Where two or more ApplyOnline applications are proceeding at the same time, brokers are to ensure loan products and repayment types are loaded consistently across all the related / linked loan applications.  For example, where one application contains an interest only loan product, the second or additional application(s) must also show any IO loan repayments being calculated on a P&I basis over the amortising period after the expiry of the I/O term.


Loan variation serviceability requirements

Where a serviceability assessment is made as part of the loan variation activities, serviceability will be assessed on P&I repayment basis and calculated over the residual loan amortising term (i.e. the P&I term).

A serviceability assessment is to be completed as part of the following loan variation activities:
  • Loan increases
  • Extension of interest only period
  • Switch from Principal and Interest (P&I) repayment type to Interest Only (IO) repayments
  • Requests to remove name from a fixed rate loan
  • Extension of loan term
(Switches from Portfolio to LIS are not permitted. Where a request is received, applications are to follow the standard new loan origination process .)

Before proceeding with a serviceability assessment for a loan variation activity, you should ensure that the application is within permitted LVR’s or product restrictions.

 

2.2 Co-borrower applications

It is contrary to Credit Policy to add a borrower to any loan facility to increase servicing/borrowing power.
Furthermore, guarantor’s incomes may not be added to applicants' incomes when assessing servicing capacity (in some cases a guarantor may be a common debt reducer ).
An acceptable co-borrower application is where a benefit can be demonstrated for all parties who are entering into the loan transaction. Refer to Eligiblity policy >2.5 Co-borrower eligibility requirements for further information. 

Co-borrowing structures are not permitted where one or more of the applicants are living overseas.  No financial benefit can be demonstrated where the sole or primary income earner will continue to reside overseas post settlement, but have no ownership of the security property.

Example:

Applicant A is a holder of a temporary visa, they are currently living and working overseas (primary income earner).  The visa type is on the approved list as per Migrant Lending

Applicant B is a holder of a temporary visa, they are currently living in Australia and are not working.  The visa type is on the approved list as per Migrant Lending

The application submitted to the bank is to buy an established property for owner occupation in Australia.  The loan is requested in joint names, but the offered security will be in applicant B’s name only.

Post settlement, Applicant A will remain living and working overseas and will not reside in the Australian property.  Applicant B will live in the Australian property but will still not be in paid employment.
This loan structure is unacceptable for lending.  Applicant A receives no benefit from the transaction, they are not occupying the property post settlement.
 
 

2.3 Common Debt Reducer (CDR)

A CDR is a person who is not an applicant on the proposed loan but shares common debts and/or commitments (which may include living expenses) with one or more of the borrowers.  Typically this is for a husband and wife structure where only one of the parties is an applicant.

An individual receiving foreign income is not acceptable to be used as a CDR.

A CDR scenario should only be used when the applicant cannot support the proposed loan and the total commitment of the shared liabilities on their own.

A servicing assessment must be performed for the CDR, in addition to the normal assessment for the applicant.  All income for the CDR is to be assessed and documented (but not verified) in accordance with existing standard lending policy.  This will mean that the applicant will have to supply the CDR’s income details to be used, otherwise they cannot be assessed.

Note:
It is the broker’s responsibility to remind the applicant what information of the CDR’s they are giving to the Bank and why it is being given.  The broker should also remind the applicant that the applicant acknowledges this when they sign the Privacy Statement.

The CDR’s individual commitment cover ratio (CCR) needs to meet the minimum CCR requirement applied to the product being sought by the borrower, as per policy.  The proposed loan is not included in calculating the serviceability ratio for the CDR (since they are not an applicant), but any other liabilities held by the CDR will need to be included in calculating their servicing capacity, in addition to the existing commitments and expenses shared with the borrower.
 
 

2.4 Commitment Cover Ratio (CCR)

The applicant(s) capacity to service all debt is rated using the Commitment Cover Ratio measurement (CCR).  The CCR is calculated by dividing all ongoing repayment commitments on the applicants’ total current and proposed debt(s) into the applicants’ income after deducting tax and living expenses.  The result is quoted as 'times’ coverage'.

Minimum CCRs will vary depending on the product, security or borrower type and may be altered from time to time.

2.5 Benchmark, buffer and floor rates

Definitions


  Benchmark rate  		A benchmark rate is an interest rate used by the Bank, to assess a customer’s ability to repay over the term of the loan.  This rate is higher than the actual product interest rate.  There are different benchmark interest rates for each home loan product.
The product benchmark rate is determined by adding a buffer rate to the respective St.George, Bank of Melbourne and BankSA standard variable rate (inclusive of the packaged discount or interest rate adjustment).
If the St.George, Bank of Melbourne and BankSA standard variable rate (inclusive of packaged discount or interest rate adjustment) plus buffer, is less than the minimum floor rate, then the greater of the two rates will become the benchmark rate and will be automatically applied for serviceability calculation.
 
Example of benchmark rate calculation:
If the St.George standard owner occupier variable P&I home loan rate is 5.22% p.a. with package discount 1.32% p.a., Bank SA 5.21% p.a. with package discount 1.31% p.a. and BoM is 5.20% p.a. with package discount 1.30% p.a., the benchmark rate will be calculated by adding the buffer rate to the respective brands home loan rate (inclusive of the packaged discount)
If the buffer rate is 3.0% p.a. then the benchmark rate for standard owner occupier variable for the respective brands will be:
  • St George: (5.22% minus 1.32%) plus 3.0% = 6.90% p.a.
  • Bank SA: (5.21% minus 1.31%) plus 3.0% = 6.90% p.a.
  • Bank of Melbourne: (5.20% minus 1.30%) plus 3.0% = 6.90% p.a.
Since the current floor rate is 5.05% p.a. which is lower than the calculated benchmark rates, the greater of the two rates (i.e. St. George 6.90% p.a., Bank SA 6.90% p.a. and Bank of Melbourne 6.90% p.a.) will become the benchmark rate and will be automatically applied for serviceability calculation.
Buffer rate The buffer rate is determined by the Bank, it reflects the potential for interest rate increases and seeks to ensure that potential increases in home loan interest rates do not adversely impact on a borrower’s capacity to repay a loan.

The Bank considers multiple economic factors and the figure is reviewed quarterly.

Effective 29 October 2021 the buffer rate is 3.0%.
Floor rate The Bank maintains a minimum floor rate, the floor rate is used in conjunction with the buffer rate.  The floor rate is the lowest benchmark rate that the Bank has set for testing a customer’s ability to repay the loan.

Effective 9 October 2020 the floor rate is 5.05% p.a.

2.6.3 External mortgage liability

External mortgage liabilities refer to loans that are not sourced from St.George Group Brands, serviceability is to be assessed on a P&I basis taken over the remaining P&I repayment term, current limit and use the maximum of either:
  • the actual interest rate being charged to the loan plus the buffer, or
  • the Bank’s floor rate.
There is no ability to over-ride the above policy for external mortgage liabilities.
Westpac and RAMS are also considered other financial institutions.
For current floor rate and buffer details refer to Benchmark, Buffer and Floor rate for details.

 


2.6.4 Remaining loan terms- internal or external debts
 

Existing debts (internal or external) are to be loaded using the remaining applicable principal and interest terms.  For example, a home loan (owner occupier or investment) should not be automatically calculated over a term of 30 years. Refer to the parameters set out in the Loan instalment calculation section.

2.10 Household expenditure measure

Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) may be used for the allocation of living expenses within an application. HEM has been developed by the Melbourne Institute to represent a broad based estimated level of absolute basic and discretionary expenditure for modern Australian households.

HEM is updated each quarter with the application processing system applying the check automatically.
 
The HEM figure is categorised according to:
  • applicants’ gross income 1
  • number of dependants (for definition of a dependant refer to Living Expenses > Rules > Definition of a dependant)
  • geographic location of the applicant’s post settlement address (at the time of application)
  • marital status.
HEM will be compared to the  expense categories listed in ‘ Customer declared living expenses compared to HEM’ and the higher of the customer declared expenses or HEM will be taken in the serviceability calculation

For incomes greater than $643k gross per annum the origination system will extrapolate the HEM value using the following calculation:
  • (Household Income amount / Mid-point of top HEM band) x (Top band HEM value – Second Top band HEM value) + Second Top band HEM value).
 
Where undistributed company profits are permitted to be used towards an individual’s serviceability calculations, the additional income will be used to determine the individual’s HEM band. 

To identify when undistributed company profits may be used for serviceability assessment refer to Self-employed applicants policy.

For the purposes of assessing an applicant’s capacity to repay the Bank, the HEM comparison outcome plus  Customer declared living expenses not compared to HEM , will be used to determine the expense figure used in the serviceability calculation.
 
The HEM comparison outcome is the higher of the below customer declared expenses or HEM. 

The total of the following expense categories and sub-categories will be compared to the HEM value.

2.14 Rental income tax deductions for Australian investment properties (Negative Gearing)

2.15 Debt to Income (DTI) Ratio
 

Loan assessment measure ‘Debt To Income Ratio’ assists Westpac Group to apply responsible lending practices in assessing a customer’s ability to service existing and proposed debts.
 


2.15.1 DTI Ratio and Calculation

 

Measure

Definition

Debt Total of the higher of limit / balance of all liabilities excluding:
  • ‘Hire purchase’, ‘Leasing’, ‘Company / Partnership / Trust liabilities’, ‘Business Loans not belonging to a sole trader’, any 'Other - Outgoing' liabilities

e.g. Debt = $500,000
 
Income Total Gross unshaded annual income (including gross rental income)

e.g. Income = $65,000
 
DTI Ratio Calculation Debt ÷ Income
 
e.g. $500,000 (Debt) ÷ $65,000 (Income) = 7.69 DTI ratio
 
 



2.15.2 High DTI Ration - Commentary and Referral to Credit

Our loan processing systems will return a DTI ratio Credit referral rule where:
  • the DTI ratio is greater than or equal to seven (7) and less than ten (10) and:
    • the LVR is greater than 80%
  • the DTI ratio is greater than or equal to ten (10)
In all instances where the DTI ratio is equal to or greater than seven (7), brokers/assessors must provide appropriate notes and comments in their submission to the effect that:  
  • They have discussed the level of debt to income with the applicant(s) noting the debt level appears relatively high
  • They have identified any specific circumstances that are contributing to the high DTI result, such as a relocation interest capitalising loan or other income not able to be included in the DTI calculation
  • Where any other policy rules have been triggered in addition to DTI, that valid mitigants can be demonstrated
  • They have validated the customers’ ability to meet repayments on the proposed loan noting the high DTI ratio
  • They have documented any other relevant rationale to support why the deal should still be approved
 
Where the application triggers a Credit referral, Credit will review the application with consideration applied to:
  • the above comments
  • the LVR, level of monthly surplus, credit history (including anything adverse on their credit bureau) and level of applicant income
  • any other policy exceptions or rules triggered

 

2.15.3 DTI and Peak Debt calculation for Relocation (bridging) loans

DTI requires us to calculate and record in ApplyOnline the Peak debt for the interest capitalising loan portion of our Relocation Loans where there is an end debt. The peak debt of the capitalising loan is the total of the limit plus interest capitalised over the full term of the capitalising loan. 
 
Serviceability is based on the end debt loan and is not required for Relocation loans with Nil end debt. 
For an applicant who has an existing bridging loan (with interest capitalisation) provided by another lender (including Westpac and RAMS) and SBG are assessing a separate standard loan for them, then the peak debt is the amount confirmed as per current policy of ‘ Commitments > 2.3 Confirm the details of the commitment’

Debt To Income Ratio is a loan assessment measure which assists the Westpac Group to apply responsible lending practices in assessing a customer's ability to service existing and proposed debts.
 

2.15.1 DTI definition and calculation

 
DTI = total debt / total gross annual income
  • Total debts based on the higher of limit or balance of all liabilities (but ignoring Hire purchase, Leasing, Company / Partnership / Trust liabilities, Business Loans not belonging to a sole trader, any 'Other - Outgoing' liabilities)
  • Total Gross unshaded annual income (including gross rental income)
Clas will automatically calculate a DTI ratio and refer applications to Credit where the DTI is greater than or equal to seven (7) times.

Example of DTI calculation
  • A customer has total debts = $500,000 and Total Gross unshaded annual income (including gross rental income) of $65,000
  • DTI calculation is $500,000 / $65,000. DTI ratio = 7.69x
  • In this example the message 'Debt to Gross Income Ratio greater than or equal to 7x-CBC'  will be triggered and the loan application would need to be referred to Credit for consideration.
