1. About this policy


1.1 Why this policy

 This policy outlines the requirements for making enquiries and capturing living expense information for each applicant that is contractually bound to repay the loan.
The customer declared living expenses are used by the Bank as part of the unsuitability assessment as required under our Responsible Lending obligations. 
This policy is applicable where the loan activity is a:
  • New loan application
  • Loan increase
  • Any servicing activity requiring a serviceability assessment
You may also need to refer to other lending policy rules, including but not limited to:
  • Commitments
  • Serviceability assessment
  • Validation of information provided by the customer
If the loan application requires mortgage insurance, refer to  Lenders mortgage insurance (LMI) where required.
 
  

2. Rules you must apply


2.1 Living expenses - Overview

 Living expenses materially affect the ability of a residential mortgage borrower to meet repayments due on a loan and are a key component of a serviceability assessment.
As part of the loan application process applicants are required to declare all household living expenses as at time of the application, as well as those which will be in place after settlement of their loan.

Example
Our applicants have an owner occupier home loan with the Bank, the new application relates to the purchase of an investment property being funded by a new Investment Home Loan.  When assessing the applicant’s serviceability we need to consider not only the new mortgage repayments that will be incurred, but also expenses relating to the new investment property.
 

2.2 Capturing customer declared living expenses

It is critical that an appropriate level of enquiry is made in order to ensure an accurate view of the applicant’s declared living expenses are captured as part of the application process.

Living expenses must be provided by the applicant and entered according to the set expense category (see table below). It is not acceptable to 'bundle' across expense categories.

The Broker is to assist the applicants by providing description of each expense category to ensure the applicants responses clearly align to our requirements.  Refer to Expense Category table .

Brokers cannot suggest a value on the applicant's behalf, except w hen rental income used in the serviceability assessment is rental income (in AUD) from a property rented on a short term basis. If rental expenses relate to a property rented on a short term basis refer to: Rental expenses from a property rented on a short term basis

In all cases also see:
  • Expense category table
  • Conflicting information
  • Validation of information provided by the customer
    • Validation of information
    • Document review – what to look for
 

2.3 Reducing living expenses

We acknowledge that a customer may change their spending behaviour following a change in circumstances (e.g. kids moving out of parent’s house) or when they have a new mortgage repayment obligation.
 
We accept that such change often involves a reduction in discretionary (non-essential) expenses such as recreation, entertainment, clothing, personal care and groceries. It is less likely that a customer can easily reduce expenses that are more stable by nature such as insurances, property-related costs, utilities, education and telephone.
 
Where the customer has told us that they are prepared to reasonably change their lifestyle to reduce living expenses, we may adjust customer declared expense amounts in the loan assessment system provided:
  • Broker has had a robust discussion with customer around their living expenses patterns; and
  • The reduction appears reasonable; and
  • Overall comments are recorded in ApplyOnline detailing the customer discussion including the reasons why the reductions were deemed acceptable by the broker.


2.4 Conflicting information

After the customer’s declared living expenses have been captured, we may need to make further enquiries with the applicant if:
  • during the assessment of the application we identify information which conflicts with the customer declared non-discretionary expenses. The conflict may be identified using data from Lender Assist  (where applicable) or other supporting documents provided by the applicant(s).  Refer to and follow the requirements as per Validation of information provided by the customer policy > Document review – what to look for (table) > Any inconsistencies or omissions in customer declared expenses where the expense type is non-discretionary; or
  • where the declared living expenses < 70% of HEM, Brokers / Assessors must refer to and follow the requirements as per Serviceability Assessment policy > Customer declared living expenses are < 70% of HEM
 

2.5 Household expenditure measure (HEM)

For the purposes of assessing an applicant’s capacity to repay the Bank, the higher of Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) and Customer Declared Expenses compared to HEM, plus Customer declared living expenses not compared to HEM, will be used in serviceability assessment. Refer to Serviceability Assessment > Rules > Household Expenditure Measure (HEM) and Customer declared living expenses compared to HEM.

For further information refer to Serviceability Assessment > Rules:

  • Household Expenditure Measure (HEM)
  • Customer declared living expenses compared to HEM.
  • Customer declared living expenses not compared to HEM

A review rule will apply when the 'Customer’s declared living expenses compared to HEM are <70% of HEM. Refer to Serviceability Assessment >  C ustomer declared living expenses < 70% of HEM for further information.

 

2.6 Factors affecting HEM / living expenses

Other factors which may impact living expenses are the applicant’s post settlement postcode and the number of dependants they have.  It is a mandatory requirement that these details are captured within the assessment system. For further information regarding dependants refer to Definition of a Dependant .

Where an Approval in Principal application is submitted and the post settlement postcode is not yet known, load the applicant's current residential postcode in the post settlement postcode field.

The application must reflect the actual post settlement postcode for formal approval. 
Living expenses should be captured as whole dollars (no cents).
 

2.7 Required commentary to validate living expenses

To comply with our regulatory obligations, commentary is required within the assessment system to explain:
  • Why an expense field has been entered as $0. Refer to Expense Category Table .
  • The nature of an expense >$0 loaded under the ‘Other’ category.  Refer to Expense Category Table .
  • Conflicts with the customer declared expenses. The commentary may be per category or as an overall comment provided the overall comment contains sufficient detail to explain why the lender chose to rely on one source of data rather than the conflicting data. Refer to  Conflicting information .
  • Reduction to living expenses. The commentary may be per category or as an overall comment provided the overall comment contains sufficient detail to identify what expenses have been adjusted, by how much and why this reduction was accepted by the lender based on what the customer told them Refer to  Reducing living expenses .

2.9 Apportioning declared living expenses in loan application 

Where an application is 'joint with spouse or de facto', apportioning living expenses against these parties is not restricted (that is, it can be 50/50 or 0/100).
Where a spouse or de facto partner is not an applicant, the entire household expenses are to be assigned to the applicant.
A common debt reducer may share expenses with a borrower, refer to Serviceability Assessment > Common debt reducer for further information.
 

2.10 Treatment of outgoing child maintenance, alimony (spousal support), rent or board

While child maintenance, alimony (spousal support), rent or board may be listed under the category of 'Expense' on loan application forms, it is not treated as a living expense. For further information refer to Commitments and Serviceability Assessment policies.

 

2.11 Definition of a Dependant

A dependant is defined as:

  • a child under the age of 18 years (The child under the age of 18 years may or may not reside with the applicant); or
  • a child aged 18 years or older who lives with the applicant, receives no income from government benefits, employment or other income sources, and is totally financially reliant on the applicant (eg. A full time unemployed student, or a child with a disability living at home permanently)
where the applicant has legal responsibility for the care and welfare of the child, on either a full or part time basis. The child may or may not reside with the applicant.

Unborn children or children are not considered dependants. Variance to this rule where Serviceability Assessment > Parental leave Home Lending’ policy is applied. 

Any additional expenses for children over the age of 18 living at home need to be captured in the living expenses discussion with the customer.
If applicants are expecting to add to their family, this should be identified during completion of Responsible Lending requirements by way of question: Do you expect any significant changes to your financial situation over the next 3 years that would adversely impact your ability to meet your repayments? For further information refer to Serviceability Assessment > Rules you must apply > Changes to financial circumstance .

Where the child does not reside with the applicant it is acceptable to use an outgoing child support payment in place of including the child as a dependant for serviceability purposes  If no child support is paid or child support is paid and the child resides with the applicant the child must be included as a dependant for serviceability purposes. Any shared custody arrangements should be clearly explained in the application

The amount of any outgoing child support payment must be verified for further information refer to Commitments > Outgoing child support and alimony (spousal support).

 

2.12 Rental expenses from a property rented on a short term basis

When rental income used in the serviceability assessment is rental income (in AUD) From a property rented on a short term basis and this property was rented for the full 52 weeks of the year. The expenses declared on the rental schedule other that interest, depreciation and once off capital expenses must be used.
Where the short-term rental property has not been rented for the full 52 weeks of the year, expenses incurred when the property is not rented are to be entered into customer expenses either as an owner occupied primary or owner-occupied secondary expenses. The lender should discuss with the customer to determine whether the property is owner occupied primary or owner occupied secondary and document accordingly.
If the property is owner-occupied and has a separate area of occupancy (granny flat) being used for short term rent, the owner-occupied portion of expenses is to be separated from the investment portion. Like above, the investment portion can be determined from the rental schedule in the tax return and the owner-occupied portion can be determined through a customer conversation.


3. Frequently asked questions (FAQs)

 
Question Answer
Are regular Chrisco payments (and other similar hamper scheme arrangements) to be captured as a living expense or a commitment? Capture as an expense.
Chrisco and other hamper schemes are like a lay-buy as you have to buy your goods before you get them and can cancel the order.
In that way they are different from a credit card and buy now pay later schemes like AfterPay and ZipPay.
How do we record expenses for self-employed applicants where, for example, the company pays for some expenses like phone and internet? If lenders enter amounts as expenses, isn’t this double dipping? Self-employed applicants cannot claim personal expenses as part of the business expenses. Anything spent from company accounts and used for personal purposes needs to be called out as a separate entry in the business or company tax returns.
As personal expenses cannot legally be claimed within the business there should be no double dipping occurring.
Why do we need to include Owner Occupied or Investment Property expenses (Rates, Electricity, water etc.) when the property has not been purchased? The customer will have these ongoing expenses after they purchase the property which will impact serviceability.
Usually customers are provided estimates of rates etc on the real estate brochure.
When customers are purchasing a home, especially when they are first home buyers, the expert, being the Lender, should be discussing with them the other costs they will incur once the property has been purchased.
It is possible they have never had to pay an electricity or water bill, or considered property rates and or strata fees they would need to pay.
In discussing these additional costs with the customer, we make them aware of costs other than the loan repayment that they would need to budget for.
Are Layby payments to be captured as a living expense or a commitment? Capture as an expense.
They are different from a credit card and short term buy now pay later schemes as you have to pay for your goods in full before you receive them.

Added the following new section
2.12 Rental expenses from a property rented on a short term basis
When rental income used in the serviceability assessment is rental income (in AUD) From a property rented on a short term basis and this property was rented for the full 52 weeks of the year. The expenses declared on the rental schedule other that interest, depreciation and once off capital expenses must be used.
Where the short-term rental property has not been rented for the full 52 weeks of the year, expenses incurred when the property is not rented are to be entered into customer expenses either as an owner occupied primary or owner-occupied secondary expenses. The lender should discuss with the customer to determine whether the property is owner occupied primary or owner occupied secondary and document accordingly.
If the property is owner-occupied and has a separate area of occupancy (granny flat) being used for short term rent, the owner-occupied portion of expenses is to be separated from the investment portion. Like above, the investment portion can be determined from the rental schedule in the tax return and the owner-occupied portion can be determined through a customer conversation
