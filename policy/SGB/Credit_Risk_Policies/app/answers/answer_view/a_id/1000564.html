1. About this policy

 1.1 Why this policy

This policy outlines the parameters for assessing loan applications that involve migrant borrower(s) in a consumer mortgage application.

A migrant is defined as an applicant that has been granted one of the Australian Governments temporary or permanent residency, visa classifications for entry to Australia.

A permanent visa allows a person unrestricted working rights in Australia and they can remain in Australia indefinitely as opposed to a temporary residence visa which may allow a person to live and work in Australia for a limited time only.

If the loan application requires mortgage insurance, refer to Lenders mortgage insurance (LMI) where required.
 

2. Rules you must apply

2.1 Establishing citizenship and residency status

A customer’s citizenship, residency or non-residency status must be established at initial application stage for all consumer mortgage loans.
For applicants who are:
  • Australian citizens who reside in Australia, or out of Australia on either a temporary or permanent basis (i.e. Australian expat), refer to Eligibility policy > Australian Citizen residency status
  • New Zealand citizens, or they hold a permanent or temporary residency visa issued by the Australian government, refer to Migrant Lending
Where the above scenarios do not apply to the applicant, refer Non-Residents lending , this will include applicants who:
  • are not an Australian or New Zealand citizen, or
  • do not hold either a permanent or temporary residency visa issued by the Australian government, or
  • hold a temporary visa issued b the Australian government, but reside out of Australia
Note: Lending to a non-resident applicant(s) is not permitted
 

2.2 Migrant lending

The purpose of the migrant lending is to provide the opportunity for eligible migrants to obtain mortgage finance on their arrival in Australia.

Eligible applicants will be able to source mortgage loan products up to a maximum lending value ratio (LVR) of
  • 95% including LMI capitalisation if the visa type is permanent
  • 90% including LMI capitalisation if the visa type is temporary

Refer to Assessing Loan to Value (LVR) , Visa matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix for further information.
 

2.3 Eligibility criteria

The migrant lending policy is available to applicants meeting the criteria below:
  • The applicant(s) visa type must be listed as an approved migrant visa type within the Visa matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix
  • A copy of the migrant’s passport and visa must be obtained and held on file, and the visa number must be added to ApplyOnline notes. Refer to Process > Evidencing migrant visa for further requirements. Where the applicant:
    • Does not hold a passport, e.g. a refugee may only hold a Protection visa (subclass 866), the application may be referred to Credit for further consideration.
    • Has an expired passport but holds a current permanent residence visa and resides in Australia, a copy of the expired passport, supported by a copy of the current visa, is acceptable.
  • Certain migrant visa holders may not have access to all products and reference must be made to the Visa Matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix prior to providing a product.
  • Applicants who are permanent residents living overseas purchasing an investment property must have a minimum of 12 months remaining on their Australian Visa (unless the visa does not have an expiry date)
  • Holders of a temporary visa must have a minimum of 12 months remaining on their Australian Visa. Additionally:
    • In a non-mortgage insured loan, a visa with no expiry date is considered to have met the minimum 12 month rule.
    • In a mortgage insured loan, the Mortgage Insurer may consider any referral on a case-by-case basis, outside of the above, with supporting commentary (i.e. visa does not have an expiry date for visa 309 and 820 with confirmation of current status)
  • If eligible to refinance existing debts, confirm satisfactory loan repayment history refer to Commitments
  • Security is to be a registered 1 st party mortgage over the property being purchased
  • Where it is identified the Temporary residency visa holder earns foreign income, the applicant is not eligible for lending and the application is unable to proceed .
  • For Permanent and Temporary residency visa holders, Australian or New Zealand citizens the valuation decision in the origination system is to be followed
  • Normal credit underwriting standards including serviceability and verification rules apply
 

2.4 Income and employment verification

  • If income is earnt in AUD:
    • Verify the AUD income as per Verifying Income policy.
  • If any foreign income is declared and required for servicing refer to:
    • Visa matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix to confirm application can proceed and that the applicant's employment status and income are compliant with the visa matrix
    • Verifying income > Rules:
      • Verifying foreign income and employment
      • Foreign Income sources and conditions
  1. Applicant(s) that has foreign income and employment are required to demonstrate they hold an appropriate visa allowing them to reside and work within the foreign country as per Verifying Income > Rules > Foreign income sources and conditions .
  2. Foreign income will impact the maximum LVR applied, eligibility to access mortgage insurance, loan purpose (e.g. refinance / cash out exclusions) and product availability refer to Verifying Income > Rules > Foreign income sources and conditions and Assessing Loan to Value (LVR) for further details.
  3. Foreign self-employed income is an unacceptable income type.
  • Applicant’s employment details are to be verified using standard income verification policy outlined in Verifying Income , and if applicable, mortgage insurance verification rules will additionally apply. If available, tax returns over the last two years or additional employment/income documentation (e.g. copy of employment contract) should be obtained to assist in the approval process 
 

2.5 Funds to complete / own contribution validation

Some migrant visa holders as per Visa matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix , must provide bank statement evidence of their funds to complete/own contribution.
Funds to complete or own contribution is generally considered to be the shortfall between the purchase price (inclusive of legal and stamp duty) and the loan being provided by the Bank.
Statements are sought from the applicants so the Bank can confirm where the monies are coming from and that the source of the funds is a genuine bank account.
  • Obtain and review the bank statements
    • Confirm the customer has the funds to complete the transaction and
    • The account holder is the same as the applicant. If the account is not held by the applicant(s), establish who is the account holder and their relationship to the applicant. Record this information in ApplyOnline assessment notes. Where the statement is not in the name of the applicant(s) the file is to be referred to Credit for consideration.
    • Copies of the bank statement(s) are to be retained in the loan file
  • Where the bank statements are in a language other than English or a currency other than Australian dollars, refer to and follow the requirements in Document translation
Note: Where the account statement(s) does not evidence the full funds to complete / own contribution, it is acceptable to proceed, where a satisfactory explanation is entered in application supporting notes, e.g. the applicant has paid a deposit on the property being purchased and where combined with funds evidenced via account statements, confirms funds to complete / own contribution.
  

2.6 Document translation

In the event foreign income and related supporting documents are not in English or display a currency other than AUD, refer to Document Standards policy > When supporting documents are not in English or display a currency other than AUD.

Applications are to contain both the original, translated documents and the translation checklist.

 

2.7 Applications with multiple applicants and varying visa types, citizenship or residency status

Where there are joint/multiple applicants, all applicants must be eligible as per policy requirements, regardless of their contribution to the loan. I.e. if the applicant type is not acceptable to the bank then the individual is unable to be a borrower, guarantor or owner of the security property.

If the application involves a migrant, check the visa type. Brokers are to confirm if the:
  • Visa type is permanent or temporary
  • Applicant’s employment status and income are compliant with the visa matrix
  • Visa is acceptable to the Mortgage Insurers, where application is mortgage insured
  • The LVR may be restricted based on the visa type of the co-applicant.
  • The maximum LVR for a temporary visa holder is 90% inclusive of any capped mortgage insurance premium providing the visa is on both the Bank’s and the Mortgage insurers approved visa list.
  • Where one of the applicants holds a temporary or permanent visa and where the visa type is not on the Bank’s approved list, the application is to be declined.
  • Where one of the applicants holds a temporary or permanent visa and where the visa type is not acceptable to the mortgage insurer, then no MI is available.
  • Loan applications structured with multiple borrowers on the loan but only a single title holder are not permitted where one or more of the applicants is currently, or will continue to reside overseas post settlement. No financial benefit can be demonstrated where the sole or primary income earner will continue to reside overseas post settlement, but have no ownership of the security property. For further details refer to Serviceability > Rules > Joint borrower applications > example.
Refer to Visa matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix for restrictions affecting each applicant type and to identify if mortgage insurance is available. 

 

2.8 Approval from foreign investment review board (FIRB)

Holders of Australian temporary residency visas require approval from the Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB) prior to purchasing a property in Australia.
Where FIRB approval is required, it is the responsibility of the purchaser to ensure that approval is held.

Applicants with enquiries or doubts regarding their eligibility to purchase real estate should be referred to the FIRB see www.firb.gov.au .

For all acceptable loan purposes and where the borrower is, or will be on the title,  we must hold a copy of the FIRB approval prior to issuing a formal approval. The only allowable exception to the FIRB requirement is where the applicant is purchasing or owns a property as Joint Tenants* (50/50), with an Australian or New Zealand citizen, or a Permanent Resident, who is their spouse.   
*The exception does not apply where the property ownership is Tenants in Common (e.g. 80/20).

The loan application must be validated against the FIRB approval to ensure the application complies with the conditions under which the FIRB approval was issued.
The validation is to include, but is not limited to:
  • Applicants name
  • Property being purchased or refinanced
  • Loan type (owner occupied v’s investment)
  • Use of property (e.g. used as principle place of residence, as confirmed with post settlement address matching property address identified in FIRB approval)
  • Rental income (e.g. if renting of the property is not permitted, then this income can’t be used for serviceability calculations)
Where the applicant lives and works overseas and FIRB indicates approval for principal place of residence # , then:
  1. The property is to be their principal place of residence while in Australia.
  2. The property cannot be used for investment (rental) or holiday purposes.
# No rental income against this property acceptable

Note:
In some cases FIRB approval is granted to a specific development. The Developer may receive a certificate identifying that further foreign investment approval is not required when sales are made to foreign persons.

A copy of the FIRB approval issued to the developer is to be retained on file. The offered security property (for purchase or refinance) should match the property identified in the FIRB approval.
If the offered security is a ‘unit’ refer to Assessing Acceptable Securities and Security Development Register (SDR) as there may be additional restrictions applicable for larger developments (including exposure limits).

Any person purchasing real estate without FIRB approval when not qualified to do so, is liable for heavy penalties.

FIRB Frequently asked questions (FAQ)
Q1 – If an application indicates we are providing funds for the purchase of a property that is not being used as security for this transaction, do we need to obtain a copy of the FIRB for that property?
A1 – No, the bank only seeks FIRB documents in relation to a property or properties it is taking as security.

Q2 – If FIRB approval was not obtained at the time of the original purchase by our applicants, but they have subsequently obtained approval, can we proceed?
A2 – We can proceed if the FIRB document provided to us shows the applicant is compliant with the FIRB approval conditions.

 

2.9 Visa Matrix and Applicant via Resident Type Matrix Overview

The Acceptable visa matrix details the:
  • Visa subclass number
  • Category of the visa type e.g. permanent or temporary
  • If the visa type is acceptable to St.George Bank Group
  • If the visa type is acceptable to mortgage insurers
  • Acceptable employment status
  • Acceptable income type
Also refer to Unacceptable Visas.

The Applicant via resident type matrix details:
  • Additional policy rules and conditions
Migrant lending customers will only be acceptable if all conditions set out in the visa matrix (and any additional conditions listed in policy) are met.
For example:
  • Business visa types should reflect income status and documentation from self-employed within Australia.
  • Sponsored visa types should reflect income from that region, i.e. state or territory sponsored visas should reflect income earned within Australia.
  • Applicants with "significant investor" visas may be required to provide proof of minimum investments held (see conditions in visa matrix)

If a visa number is not currently on the acceptable visa list or the visa is no longer available, or you have been made aware of a new visa number (or visa sub class), then these can be referred to Credit for consideration where supported by detailed comments and mitigants.  

Note: For applications that require Mortgage Insurance, refer to the Mortgage Insurer for consideration on a case-by-case basis, with supporting comments and mitigants.


Refer to Visa matrix and Applicant via Resident Type matrix for further details.

3. Process to follow

3.1 Evidencing migrant visa

Migrant status must be evidenced via provision of a copy of a work visa approval issued by Department of Home Affairs or a printout supplied by the customer from the VEVO web page https://online.immi.gov.au/evo/firstParty?actionType=query  
Where an applicant’s migrant category is an Investor Visa type, evidence is to be provided of approved deposit funds held with State Treasury.
Broker and BMS Assessors are responsible for verifying migrant specific documentation and confirmation that the visa type is acceptable. Visa details must be recorded in ApplyOnline notes and copies submitted with loan application.
 

3.2 Loan application

  • Where directed by the Applicant via resident type matrix, brokers must ‘tick’ the Non Resident Lending flag in the Personal Details section of ApplyOnline
  • In all cases the broker is to enter notes that the application is being submitted under the Migrant lending policy
  • The Visa number and classification type must be recorded in ApplyOnline notes and copies of the applicant’s passport and visa submitted with application documents
  • Where the applicant is yet to locate a property in Australia to purchase, applications will be initially processed as Approval in Principal
  • All loans for temporary visa holders requiring Mortgage Insurance are to be referred to WLMI for review.
