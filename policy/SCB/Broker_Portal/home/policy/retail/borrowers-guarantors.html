Borrowers & Guarantors

Why

It is important to clarify who is and who isn’t eligible for a loan and under what circumstances a guarantor may be considered.

What

The legal structure of the customer must be identified upon receipt of the loan application.

Eligible home loan borrowers include:

• Individuals over the age of 18,

• Proprietary companies, and

• Trustees where the trust is a legal entity (except trustees of Self- managed Superannuation Funds).

Foreign nationals and non-residents may be eligible borrowers but pose a greater credit risk and have restrictions on approval criteria. Refer to Lending to Non-Residents.

Unacceptable Borrowers can be found here.

The Banking Code of Practice requires responsible lenders to ensure joint or co-borrowers receive a substantial benefit from the proceeds of a loan. Refer to Joint or Co-Borrowers.

Some lending arrangements involve accepting security from third parties. Third party security providers are guarantors, not co-borrowers.
Lending against guarantees poses credit risks that are greater than normal and involve specific requirements that need to be followed. Refer to Guarantees as Security.

Duty of disclosure

Brokers are required to disclose any financial interests they have in a loan application.

For the purpose of this guide a self-employed customer is defined as any person or entity who:

  • Derives more than 25% of their total gross income from a business in which they hold ownership or from investment related sources, OR
  • Holds an active ABN regardless of whether they are registered with the Australian Taxation Office for GST purposes.

A resident borrower is defined as a person holding Australian citizenship OR a visa or other right to reside in Australia indefinitely.

Loans are not provided to non-residents apart from Australian citizens that are temporarily living overseas

Specific requirements that apply to non-resident borrowers temporarily living overseas OR residents that do not have a permanent right to reside in Australia are set out below.

Security

Maximum Loan to Valuation Ratio (LVR) is 70%. Third party security is not acceptable.

Currency Risk

It is preferred that loan repayments are met by income derived in Australia.

Where income from overseas is relied upon, potential fluctuations in foreign exchange rates create risk. Consequently, not more than 70% of the AUD equivalent of income received on a consistent basis from overseas may be included in Capacity to Repay (CTR) calculations, subject to:

  • it being verified as personal income, e.g. salary/ wage, pensions/ superannuation (foreign business income is not acceptable),
  • the income having been consistently received for at least six months,
  • currency being listed on Suncorp Bank’s current “SML Buys Inward TTs Rate” list (see Acceptable Currencies), and
  • the conversion to AUD being conducted by referring to the rate table applicable to the date of the CTR calculation.

Documentation is to evidence:

  • employment terms,
  • income amount and currency type,
  • payment frequency.

Documentation in foreign languages will not be accepted unless it is supported by a certified English language translation and the accuracy of the translated text can be confirmed by a Suncorp Bank employee.

Facility Structuring

All loans are to be structured on principal and interest repayment arrangements from the outset with a maximum term of 25 years.

Power of Attorney

All non-resident borrowers are to grant to a Solicitor located and practicing in Australia a Power of Attorney for acceptance of notices and to execute loan documents in the event the customer is unable to be contacted.

The following borrower types are unacceptable. Any person who:

  • is under the age of 18 years at the time of the loan application,
  • is engaged in an illegal activity,
  • does not have the legal capacity to enter a contract,
  • is currently serving a prison sentence,
  • is an undischarged bankrupt,
  • has a ban on their Credit Bureau information,
  • has had debt forgiven by other financiers/ creditors, or
  • Suncorp Bank has lent to and subsequently incurred a loss.

The Banking Code of Practice requires responsible lenders to ensure joint or co-borrowers receive a substantial benefit from the proceeds of a loan. 

For the purpose of this guide a substantial benefit to an individual is: 

  1. When a proportionate legal (direct) or equitable (indirect) interest is acquired in assets purchased with the loan proceeds, or 
  2. A proportionate amount of the loan funds is to be used to repay the joint or co- borrower’s debts or other obligations. 

Unless it is clearly evident from the loan purpose, you must record in your loan submission notes the substantial benefit to be obtained by a co-borrower who is NOT BOTH: 

  • a security provider (mortgagor), AND 
  • in a spousal relationship and cohabitating with the co-borrower. 

When considering the borrower's capacity to repay a regulated loan, the guarantor's income:

  • may only be used to evidence capacity to meet the guarantor's commitments and living expenses, and
  • cannot be used to support the existing or proposed commitments of the borrower as part of the assessment of the borrower’s capacity to repay, unless the  (refer definition below) of the parties is undoubted (e.g. husband and wife), in which case the guarantor's income may be used to offset their portion of joint commitments and household expenses.

Financial interdependence is defined as persons in a spousal (or equivalent) relationship AND co-habiting AND providing each other with financial or domestic support or personal care.

Circumstances where taking a guarantee may be appropriate or required include:

Marital/ De Facto Relationship

Where the borrowers and security providers are not identical, but they are financially interdependent.

Where the borrower needs to rely on the income of a financially interdependent person (who is not a borrower) to offset the other person’s portion of joint expenses and liabilities in order demonstrate Capacity to Repay (CTR).

Companies

It is preferable that guarantees are obtained from all directors and shareholders. However, guarantees may be waived in instances where:

  • the shareholder is not a director; and
  • the holding is no greater than 20%; and
  • the shareholder does not have effective control through voting rights. 

Trusts

Guarantees are to be taken from:

  • The trustee both in its own right and in its capacity as trustee.
  • All adult beneficiaries of Discretionary Trusts.
  • Adult or corporate beneficiary holders of more than 20% of the units in Unit Trusts.

It is preferable that guarantees are obtained in all instances. However, guarantees may be waived in instances where the beneficiaries:

  • receive 20% or less of total distributions from the trust;
  • are not key decision makers/ influencers or hold key management positions in the business.

All adult beneficiaries must agree to the Trust providing the guarantee even if they do not provide their own personal guarantee.

Legal Advice

Guarantors must obtain independent legal advice and provide a Solicitor’s Certificate, the relevant State’s Law Society Certificate, or Statutory Declaration.  It is recommended that they also seek financial advice.

Evidence of independent legal advice must be executed prior to the guarantee. *No exceptions will be provided for this condition.

If a Guarantor refuses to obtain legal advice or obtains legal advice from the Borrower’s solicitor, the application will not proceed.
